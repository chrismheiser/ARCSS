Our Team

Our interdisciplinary team includes faculty, postdoc's and students with a wide range of backgrounds who are working together to understand how climate influences glaciated watersheds and is represented by sedimentation in Arctic glacier-fed lakes. The team includes experts in Arctic watershed hydrology, fluvial geomorphology, lake sedimentation, and glaciology. We integrate modern process studies, paleo perspectives, and modeling approaches.

Graduate Students


Rebecca Ellerbroek

Northern Arizona University

Rabecca is a graduate student in NAU's geology program, where she is using water isotopes to determine relative contributions of rain, snow, and glacial melt into an Arctic lake system. Rebecca received her bachelor's degree in art and geology from Cornell College in Iowa; her undergraduate research investigated ENSO effects on monsoon precipitation using Australian speleothems.

Stacy Kish

Northern Arizona University

Stacy is a Doctoral student in the School of Earth Sciences and Environmental Sustainability at NAU. She received her Master’s degree from Oregon State University where she examined ocean sediment to reconstruct climate in the eastern Equatorial Pacific for the past 160 thousand years. Stacy's current research focuses on developing the paleoclimate record from sediment obtained from Lake Peters, located in the Brooks Range of Alaska.

Johnse Ostman

Alaska Pacific University

Johnse is pursuing his masters degree in the environmental science program at alaska pacific university extracurricular to working full-time with the USGS. he received his bs degree in environmental science and mathematics from the University of Alaska-Southeast where he focused on interactions between surface water hydrology and glacier processes. johnse’s current research aims to validate WASIM as an operational hydrological forecast model for the glacierized Eklutna basin in Alaska.

Ethan Yackulic

Field Team


Cody Routson

Northern Arizona University

Cody researches, developes, and uses natural climate archives to extend observational records, establish baseline conditions, and characterize the climate system and risks of climate-related natural hazards. His post-doctoral research is focused on Northern Hemisphere climate drivers, including Arctic weather patterns and sea ice fluctuation. Arctic sea-ice loss has major implications for both local and hemispheric climate. Cody's work helps characterize the risk of extreme events and inform adaptation strategies to cope with climate change.

Doug Steen

Northern Arizona University

Doug is a Master’s student in the Geology program at NAU. His research interests include interpreting recent climatic and environmental changes from proxies within lacustrine sediment. Doug's current research focuses on developing a record of paleomagnetism and environmental magnetism for sediment cores from Cascade and Shainin lakes in the north-central Brooks Range of Alaska.
